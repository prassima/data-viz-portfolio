  • Bars provide an intuitive shape for viewers to grasp the relative size of categories.
  • Ranking categories using a logical sequence (i.e. magnitude) aides the viewer in interpreting the content of the chart.
  • Use colour purposefully to help guide the viewer to identify the key takeaway.
  • Chart headers should be used to provide an analysis of a chart's content. However, if the viewers do not know what the data is about, it can be helpful to contextualise the data using a header.
  • While lines are a great way to display trends, sometimes long trend lines can create noise for the viewer. If the main takeaway is about a change between two specific time frames, it is sufficient to include data on only those two periods.

  • Providing viewers with control over what the chart includes can drive engagement and curiosity, but beware of overstimulating your viewers.
  • Using a reference line or shading a specific area are both effective means for providing important context.

  • Intentional use of labels or colours on charts with many points, such as a scatterplot, helps to orientate the viewer's focus.

I find it particularly rewarding when a chart can convey not just the data, but also delivers a meta message.

For instance, visualising the long term trend of growing carbon emissions using an area chart is not only effective - it also creates a visual metaphor, where the chart itself resembles pollution clouds.
