Shapes matter ▼

Bars provide an intuitive shape for viewers to grasp the relative size of categories

Logical ordering ▼

Ranking categories using a logical sequence (i.e. magnitude, alphabetical) aides the viewer in interpreting the content of the chart

Intentional colours ▼

Use colour purposefully to help guide the viewer to identify the key takeaway

Headers ▼

Chart headers should provide an analysis of a chart’s content. However, if the visual will have multiple low frequency viewers, the header can prioritise contextualising the data.

Line charts ▼

While line charts with a timeframe on the x-axis are a great way to display data over time, sometimes long trend lines can create noise for the viewer. If the main takeaway is about a change between two specific points, only including data for those two points can communicate the message more clearly.

User controls ▼

Providing users with control over what they see, like the drop-down you've just expanded facilitates, can drive engagement and curiosity, but beware of overstimulating your viewers.

Reference lines ▼

Using a reference line or shading a specific area are both effective means for providing important context.

Purposeful labels ▼

Intentional use of labels or colours on charts with many points, such as a scatterplot, helps to orientate the viewer's focus.

Meta design ▼

I find it particularly rewarding when a chart can convey not just the data, but also delivers a meta message. For instance, visualising the long term trend of growing carbon emissions using an area chart is not only effective - it also creates a visual metaphor, where the chart itself resembles pollution clouds.
